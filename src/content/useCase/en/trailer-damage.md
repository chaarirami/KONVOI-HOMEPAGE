## Unresolved Damage: A Systemic Problem in Freight Forwarding

Trailer damage is a fact of life in goods transport. What becomes a problem in many cases is not the damage itself but the question: when did it occur? And who bears liability?

Without reliable data, this question can rarely be answered definitively. Carriers, shippers, subcontractors and consignees have different interests and different accounts of the same event. The result is protracted insurance disputes, unresolved liability claims and ultimately out-of-court settlements in which the fleet operator bears part of the cost of damage they did not cause.

## Typical Damage Scenarios Without a Data Basis

The most common damage occurs at loading docks: a forklift or floor conveyor hits the trailer wall, doors or floor during loading or unloading. These collisions are brief and localised — without sensor data it cannot be established after the fact whether the damage occurred during loading at the origin, unloading at the destination or at an intermediate stop en route.

Road vibrations on poor surfaces can also cause consequential damage to sensitive cargo or already weakened trailer infrastructure. Low-speed collisions during manoeuvring, where the impact is too minor to prompt a report, leave their mark. In subcontractor transport and intermodal chains, where the trailer is moved by multiple parties, the evidentiary position is particularly thin.

## Insurance Disputes Without Evidence Are Costly

The financial consequences of unresolved trailer damage are substantial. A typical bodywork repair on a trailer costs €2,000 to €15,000. If consequential damage to the cargo is involved, total costs can quickly reach six figures. Insurers frequently reject claims without clear evidence or significantly reduce the settlement.

For fleet operators this means: they pay insurance premiums for protection they can rarely invoke in a dispute. And with every unresolved incident, the risk of premium increases at the next contract renewal grows.
